Totem Worksite Lighting

Construction workers describe intense glare, contrasty shadows, and lack of control with current worksite lighting solutions. Giving workers better visibilty of texture and depth on the ground increases safety and confidence when working at night.

A conversation with operators

"The balloon lights they use are really bright, but they cast really dark shadows, so you can either see or you're completely blind."
— CAT Operator

"A lot of the time, instead of using a single lighting plant, we'll just turn on the lights on an excavator and point it at the work site".
— CAT Operator

"The lights on vehicles are bright, but they only point forward and backward, so if you're filling a dump truck with a loader, you won't see the truck until you point the loader at it."
— CAT Operator

"When you're digging with an excavator at night, the lights are plenty until you've dug far enough that the lights just cast shadows into your trench. Then it's just an ocean of pitch black in there."
— CAT Operator

Brainstorming

To start, we combed our research for potential directions. Anything from mountable fixtures, remote controlled systems, or dedicated lighting vehicles.

Operators responded well to mountable fixtures since they could be effective for vehicles as well as workers on foot. How can we make a mountable fixture which can be easily moved, but is also large enough to diffuse the light and soften shadows?

Mockup

The design we created on is a cylindrical diffuser which would be mounted anywhere magnetically. It could also be chained with other units to create an entire column of light.

Timeline

2005

Carnegie Mellon Pre-college for Design – Pittsburgh, PA

In the summer of 2005, I thought I'd get my toes wet in design at my brother's alma mater, Carnegie Mellon. It was the most comprehensive introduction to this field I could have ever hoped for, but I still wanted to approach creativity as a technical professional.

2007

Photography Assistant, Visual Waves Commercial Photography – New York, NY

In 2007, impatient to be move on from high school, I grabbed an opportunity to assist a commercial photographer named William Vazquez.

2007

Purdue University, Computer Science – West Lafayette, IN

I had been writing code since my sophomore year in high school. Passionate about computers and the web, I saw computer science as a way of bringing together making and code in a neat package.

2008

Intern, WinMill Software – New York, NY

Even after a year studying computer science at Purdue, I still needed some insight into what I would really be doing in the industry. WinMill gave me independence, an awareness of how many people depend on a single deadline, and a lot of room to explore my skills as a programmer.

2009

Switched to Industrial Design

After a lot of soul searching, I realized that computer science wasn't fulfilling my interest in solving actual human problems. I felt as though I was being trained in how to use technology, not in how apply technology to solve real world problems. I made the switch that changed my life.

2012

Intern, Streng Design – New York, NY

I spent this summer designing handles for a hush hush new appliance design for PepsiCo.

2013

Junior Designer, Streng Design – New York, NY

I had to come back to Streng. It was a small environment where I could really spread my wings. Sketching, CAD, renderings, concept development. I ate up as much work as I could find to do.

2014

UX Designer, IBM Bluemix – Austin, TX

I originally came to IBM to practice industrial design for enterprise servers. But early on, I was confronted with the opportunity to move over to IBM Design, a budding, design-led movement to modernize IBM's entire software product development process. Suddenly, my job title changed to UX Designer, and I found myself as not only a designer, but also as an educator and advocate helping steer the colossal, inertial ship that is IBM toward design thinking and user focused innovation.
